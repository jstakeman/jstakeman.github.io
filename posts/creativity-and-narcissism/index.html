Creativity and Narcissism

Where I grew up, being of service is the primary measure of value. This sentiment works for both people and things. A pristine gold encrusted shovel locked behind glass is not valuable. A genius spinster writing her days away only gains value once her work is published. Industry for its own sake is vanity. These are the rules laid out by a radical, and possibly militant, pragmatism.

The most productive creative people I know are driven primarily by personal passion. Some are escaping demons, others seem to produce almost by reflex. That powerful passion though is the most pervasive throughline in my admittedly somewhat limited sample set.

These beautiful, creative minds do their work because it is important to them. That internal engine leads them to do great things if only to scratch that itch of theirs. It effortlessly fuels late night hours or manic sprints and bursts of creativity.

Mine sputters. At every step along the way the dampers of "so what" choke off the oxygen. I have literally worked for free sweeping out trailers while working for a man who would email us about that football team in Denver he owned. There was no question in my mind in picking up that broom, but I sometimes have to build myself up to sitting down in a comfy chair with a laptop.

What gives? That makes zero sense to me and yet it is often my reality.

There must be a better way. Some sort of Nash equilibrium where the optimal path is service to oneself and to others. I stumble across it sometimes but couldn't point it out to you on a map.
